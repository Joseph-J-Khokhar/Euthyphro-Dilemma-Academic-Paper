# The Euthyphro Dilemma and the Grounding of Morality

## Overview

This paper examines the Euthyphro dilemma as presented in Plato’s *Euthyphro*, which raises a fundamental question in metaethics and philosophy of religion:

> Is something morally good because God commands it, or does God command it because it is already morally good?

This question challenges Divine Command Theory (DCT) by introducing two major concerns:
- **Arbitrariness** (if morality depends entirely on divine will)
- **Moral independence** (if morality exists independently of God)

The paper evaluates these challenges and focuses on Robert Adams’ modified Divine Command Theory, which grounds moral truth in the necessarily loving nature of God rather than in arbitrary divine commands.

---

## Research Question

Can morality be coherently grounded in God’s nature without becoming either arbitrary or independent of God?

---

## Thesis

While classical Divine Command Theory faces serious problems raised by the Euthyphro dilemma, Robert Adams’ modified account significantly strengthens the theistic position by grounding morality in God’s essential nature. However, the theory still faces philosophical pressure concerning whether divine nature genuinely explains moral truth or merely re-describes it.
